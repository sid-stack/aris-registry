# The Bid/No-Bid Decision Framework Every Government Contractor Needs

**Target keyword:** bid no bid decision framework
**Publish:** Week 1

---

Most government contractors lose before they write a single word.

Not because their proposal was weak. Because they bid on the wrong opportunity.

The RFP looked relevant. The NAICS code matched. The agency was familiar. So they spent 80 hours building a response — and lost to an incumbent who had the contract locked before the solicitation dropped.

This is the most expensive mistake in federal contracting. And it's completely avoidable.

---

## What a Bid/No-Bid Framework Actually Is

A bid/no-bid framework is a structured scoring system you apply to every new opportunity before committing proposal resources.

It forces a single question: **Is the probability of winning worth the cost of bidding?**

That cost is real. A mid-size proposal runs 200–400 hours of internal labor. At blended rates, that's $15,000–$40,000 per response. If your win rate is 20%, you're spending $75,000–$200,000 in proposal costs for every contract you win.

A rigorous bid/no-bid process cuts that waste in half.

---

## The 6-Factor Scoring Model

Score each factor 1–5. A total score under 18 is a no-bid. Above 24 is a strong bid. Between 18–24 requires a strategic discussion.

### 1. Incumbent Status (weight: high)
- 5 — You are the incumbent
- 3 — No incumbent, true open competition
- 1 — Competitor is the incumbent with strong performance record

Incumbents win 70–80% of re-competes in federal contracting. If a competitor owns the work and there are no performance issues, your baseline win probability is under 15%.

### 2. Past Performance Alignment
- 5 — You have 3+ directly relevant contracts at this scope and dollar value
- 3 — You have adjacent past performance you can position
- 1 — No past performance at this agency or in this domain

The government will compare your past performance to the requirement. If you can't demonstrate you've done this before, you'll lose on this factor alone.

### 3. Relationship Capital
- 5 — You know the program office, have met the CO, attended industry days
- 3 — You have contacts at the agency but not this program
- 1 — No prior agency touch at all

Contracts are awarded by people. Relationship capital directly translates to win probability. If you've never spoken to anyone at this agency, you're competing blind.

### 4. Teaming Position
- 5 — You are prime with strong subs locked in
- 3 — You are a competitive sub on a strong prime
- 1 — You are prime with no teaming strategy and significant gaps

Going prime with coverage gaps is a proposal killer. If the requirement has capability areas you can't fill, make sure you have the right teammates before you commit.

### 5. Pricing Intelligence
- 5 — You have IGCE data, prior award data, or competitor rate insight
- 3 — You can estimate from public awards with similar scope
- 1 — No pricing intelligence — pure guesswork

Pricing blind means you're either leaving margin on the table or pricing yourself out of contention. Both are losing outcomes.

### 6. Capture Maturity
- 5 — You've been shaping this opportunity for 6+ months
- 3 — You found this at pre-solicitation and have time to build position
- 1 — You found this at RFP release with zero prior capture activity

The best time to start a capture is 12 months before RFP drop. If you're starting at solicitation release, you're reacting — not competing.

---

## The Rule Most Contractors Ignore

**If you don't know the answer to factors 1, 3, and 5 — don't bid.**

Those three factors — incumbent status, relationship capital, and pricing intelligence — are not things you can make up in a proposal. They require pre-solicitation work. If you're at RFP release and still blank on all three, your win probability is under 10%.

No framework can make a 10% bid worth the cost.

---

## How to Apply This in Practice

Run the framework the moment an opportunity hits your radar — not when the RFP drops.

Track every opportunity in a pipeline with the 6-factor score updated at each milestone:
- Sources Sought / RFI
- Pre-solicitation notice
- Industry Day
- Draft RFP
- Final RFP

If your score drops at any stage, re-evaluate. Markets change. Incumbents get performance issues. Budgets shift. A no-bid at pre-solicitation can become a strong bid by Draft RFP — and vice versa.

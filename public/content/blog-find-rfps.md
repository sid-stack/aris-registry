# How to Find Government RFPs Before Your Competitors Do

**Target keyword:** how to find government RFPs
**Publish:** Week 3

---

Most contractors find RFPs the same way: they check SAM.gov every few days, see what's posted, and decide whether to bid.

That's not a pipeline strategy. That's reactive searching. And it puts you in competition with every other contractor who's doing the same thing — starting from zero at solicitation release.

The contractors who consistently win start their pipeline earlier, at lower stages of the acquisition lifecycle, where the opportunity is still being shaped. Here's how they do it.

---

## Stage 1: Find Opportunities Before They're Opportunities

The acquisition lifecycle starts long before SAM.gov. It starts in agency budget documents, program office roadmaps, and congressional appropriations.

**Where to look:**

**FPDS-NG (Federal Procurement Data System):** Every federal contract award is public. Search by NAICS code, agency, and dollar threshold. Find which agencies are spending in your space, who the incumbents are, and when their contracts expire. An expiring contract is a forthcoming recompete.

**USASpending.gov:** Same data, better interface. Filter by award type, agency, and period of performance. Set end-date alerts for contracts in your NAICS codes at your target agencies.

**Agency Forecast of Contract Opportunities:** Most agencies publish an annual procurement forecast. It lists anticipated solicitations, expected dollar values, small business designations, and planned acquisition timelines. These forecasts are published 6–18 months before RFP release.

**Budget Justification Documents (CJ/PB):** Congressional budget justifications and president's budget documents name specific programs by dollar value. If you see a $15M IT modernization program in an agency's budget submission, you know a solicitation is coming. Start your intelligence gathering now.

---

## Stage 2: Use SAM.gov for What It's Actually Good At

SAM.gov has more data than most contractors use. The problem is the search interface — it surfaces obvious results and buries the rest.

**What to search for beyond RFPs:**

- **Sources Sought notices:** These are market research notices. The government is testing whether small businesses can perform this work. Responding to a Sources Sought gets you on the program office's radar before the solicitation exists. This is where relationships start.

- **Special Notices / Industry Days:** Industry day announcements are published on SAM.gov. Attending an industry day and meeting the CO and program manager is worth more than 40 hours of proposal writing. The intelligence you gather shapes your entire capture approach.

- **Draft RFPs:** Many agencies publish a draft solicitation for public comment. Submitting substantive comments (not generic ones) puts your name in front of the contracting officer before a single proposal has been written.

- **Contract awards:** Search for awards in your NAICS codes. Find out who won, at what price, and when the period of performance ends. This tells you where your next competition is.

**Set up automated searches:** SAM.gov allows you to save searches and receive email alerts. Set alerts for your top 3–5 NAICS codes at your target agencies. Run them daily, not weekly.

## Stage 3: Build Agency Intelligence Before You Need It

The best pipeline intelligence doesn't come from databases. It comes from people.

**FOIA requests:** The Freedom of Information Act lets you request acquisition planning documents, prior contract files, and market research reports from agency contracting offices. The winning proposal from a prior competition is often available via FOIA. Reading a winning proposal from your target agency in your target domain is the most valuable competitive intelligence you can collect.

**Congressional testimony and budget hearings:** Agency heads testify before Congress about their priorities and funding levels. These transcripts are public. They tell you exactly what the agency leadership cares about — which translates directly into evaluation criteria.

**Industry associations:** AFCEA, NCMA, ACT-IAC, and sector-specific associations run events where agency program managers speak publicly about their challenges. These presentations often name upcoming acquisitions by name. Attend, take notes, follow up.

**LinkedIn signals:** Program managers and contracting officers change roles. When someone moves from PM to a new program office, their former program is likely going through transition. When an agency posts a job for a Contracting Officer in a specific program area, an acquisition is being stood up. These are weak signals, but they're early ones.

## Stage 4: Build a Pipeline That Runs Itself

Tracking opportunities manually doesn't scale. You need a system.

**Minimum viable pipeline tracker:**
- Opportunity name and SAM.gov notice ID
- NAICS code and dollar threshold
- Estimated RFP drop date
- Current acquisition stage
- Your bid/no-bid score (see our bid/no-bid framework post)
- Capture lead assigned
- Key intelligence gathered to date

Update this tracker weekly. Review it in a standing pipeline meeting — 30 minutes, every Monday, with your BD and capture leads. The meeting forces decisions: advance, pause, or drop.

**The goal:** Never be surprised by an RFP drop. Every solicitation you bid should be in your pipeline at least 90 days before release. Ideally 180 days.

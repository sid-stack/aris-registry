# 5 Signs Your RFP Process Is Killing Your Win Rate

**Target keyword:** RFP win rate improvement
**Publish:** Week 2

---

The average government contractor wins 20–25% of the proposals they submit.

The top 10% win 45–60%.

The difference isn't writing talent. It's process. Specifically, it's whether your proposal operation is built to win — or just built to submit.

Here are the five signs that your current process is working against you.

---

## Sign 1: You Start at RFP Release

If your team's first move is "the solicitation just dropped, let's build a color team schedule," you've already lost ground.

Winning proposals are built before the RFP drops. They reflect intelligence gathered during the capture phase: understanding of the customer's real priorities, knowledge of the evaluation criteria before they're published, relationships that shaped how the requirement was written.

When you start at solicitation release, you're writing a generic response to a published document. Your competitors who started six months ago are writing to a customer they know.

**The fix:** Start a capture file for every opportunity you're tracking at Sources Sought or earlier. Even 30 minutes of pre-solicitation research per opportunity compounds significantly across your pipeline.

---

## Sign 2: Your Compliance Matrix Is Built After the First Draft

The compliance matrix is not a document you create after writing the proposal. It's the blueprint you write the proposal from.

If your writers are drafting sections without a completed, section-by-section compliance matrix in front of them, you're creating rework. They'll miss requirements. They'll answer questions the government didn't ask. They'll under-address evaluation factors that actually drive the award decision.

Every proposal that goes to a color review with compliance gaps was built backwards.

**The fix:** Build the matrix on Day 1 of proposal kickoff — before any writing starts. Assign every requirement (Section L and M, PWS/SOW, CDRL items) to a proposal section. Writers work from the matrix, not from their own interpretation of the RFP.

---

## Sign 3: You Have No Pricing Strategy Until B&P

If your price team isn't involved until the last week, you don't have a pricing strategy. You have a pricing scramble.

Pricing is a win strategy decision, not a math exercise. Should you go best value on price? Should you underprice to break in? What are your competitors likely to price based on public award data? What's the IGCE — and how close is it to your cost structure?

These are capture-phase questions. If they're not answered before your technical volume is written, you'll write a proposal that's technically strong and commercially uncompetitive.

**The fix:** Assign a price-to-win lead at pre-solicitation. Their job is to gather pricing intelligence, build the PTW model, and give the proposal team a price target before the first color review.

---

## Sign 4: Every Proposal Uses the Same Boilerplate

Open your last five proposals. If the company capability section looks the same, the past performance narratives are mostly the same, and the management approach is essentially the same — you're not competing. You're reusing.

Evaluators read hundreds of proposals. They recognize boilerplate. More importantly, boilerplate doesn't speak to the specific customer's specific problem. It speaks to your general existence as a company.

Winning proposals are customized. They demonstrate understanding of this agency's mission, this program's challenges, and this contract's specific risk areas. They use the customer's own language from industry days, market research requests, and program documentation.

**The fix:** Maintain a library of versioned, modular content — but require that every section be tailored to the specific opportunity. Writers should be able to identify at least three customer-specific elements they incorporated in each major section.

---

## Sign 5: You Don't Debrief When You Lose

If you're not requesting debriefs after every loss, you're flying blind.

The government is required to provide a debrief if you ask. That debrief tells you where you ranked, how your technical score compared to the winner, where evaluators found weaknesses, and in some cases, what the winner did differently.

This is the highest-value intelligence you can collect. It tells you exactly where your process broke down — whether it was past performance, technical approach, price, or something you didn't even know was an evaluation factor.

Most contractors skip the debrief because losing is uncomfortable. The contractors who request every debrief, document the findings, and incorporate them into the next proposal are the ones who improve from 20% to 45%.

**The fix:** Make debrief requests automatic. Within 72 hours of an award notification, your BD lead submits the debrief request. The findings go into a win/loss database that informs every future proposal on similar vehicles or with the same agency.

---

## What Fixing This Actually Looks Like

Process improvements compound. Fixing your compliance matrix process reduces rework by 30%. Running debriefs and incorporating findings improves your technical score over 6–12 months. Starting earlier on pricing gives you competitive positioning you can't manufacture at the last minute.

None of this is revolutionary. The top contractors do all of it systematically. The average contractors do it inconsistently, under deadline pressure, with whoever's available.

The difference is discipline, not resources.

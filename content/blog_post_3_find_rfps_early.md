# How to Find Government RFPs Before Your Competitors Do

**URL slug:** /blog/how-to-find-government-rfps-early  
**Target keyword:** how to find government RFPs  
**Meta description:** The contractors winning the most government work aren't just responding faster — they're finding opportunities earlier. Here's how.

---

Most government contractors are reactive. An RFP drops on SAM.gov, they find it, they scramble to decide whether to bid, and they start writing under time pressure.

The contractors consistently winning large awards are operating differently. They know about opportunities weeks or months before the final solicitation is published — and they use that time to build relationships, shape requirements, and prepare.

Here's how to build an early-warning system for government opportunities.

## Step 1: Track Sources Sought and RFIs

Before an agency publishes a final RFP, they often publish a Sources Sought notice or Request for Information (RFI). These are market research tools — the agency is gauging whether qualified vendors exist and gathering input on requirements.

Responding to Sources Sought is free, relatively low-effort, and positions you as an informed player before the competition even starts. Agencies track who responds and frequently reach out during the formal procurement process.

**Where to find them:** SAM.gov → filter by "Sources Sought" and "Special Notice" under Notice Type.

## Step 2: Follow Pre-Solicitation Notices

Pre-solicitation notices announce that a procurement is coming. They're often published 30–90 days before the final RFP and include enough information to start preliminary teaming conversations and capability positioning.

Set up SAM.gov email alerts for your target NAICS codes and agencies. Don't just monitor — respond with a capability statement when appropriate.

## Step 3: Monitor Agency Forecast Tools

Many agencies publish their acquisition forecasts — planned procurements for the fiscal year — on their own websites. These forecasts often include estimated values, timelines, and incumbent information.

Key forecasts to bookmark:
- **DHS:** acquisition.gov and DHS.gov acquisition forecast
- **DoD:** defense.gov and individual service websites
- **HHS, VA, DOT:** all maintain public forecast pages
- **GSA:** GSA.gov acquisition planning

These forecasts are updated quarterly and give you a 6–12 month view of what's coming.

## Step 4: Use FPDS to Find Expiring Contracts

The Federal Procurement Data System (FPDS) tracks every federal contract award. If you know a contract is expiring in 6–12 months, you can start positioning for the recompete now.

Search FPDS for contracts in your target agencies and NAICS codes with end dates in the next 12 months. These are your highest-probability pipeline opportunities — the work exists, the budget exists, and the agency already knows they need a contractor.

**Tip:** Filter for contracts where the incumbent is a small business if you're competing in that space. Recompetes of small business contracts often see more competition.

## Step 5: Build Agency Relationships Before the Solicitation

The most effective thing you can do before an RFP drops is get in front of the contracting officer or program office. This is legal, common, and expected in government contracting.

Request an industry day meeting, submit a white paper, or respond to an RFI. Every touchpoint before the RFP increases your probability of win.

---

## The Time Advantage

If you're finding RFPs the day they're published, you have 30–60 days to respond. If you're finding the pre-solicitation 90 days earlier, you have 4–5 months to prepare a competitive response. That's not a small difference — that's a fundamentally different probability of winning.

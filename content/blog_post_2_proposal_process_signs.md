# 5 Signs Your Proposal Process Is Costing You Contracts

**URL slug:** /blog/proposal-process-costing-you-contracts  
**Target keyword:** RFP win rate improvement  
**Meta description:** If your team is doing any of these five things, your proposal process is actively hurting your win rate — and your revenue.

---

A bad proposal process doesn't announce itself. It shows up as a pattern of losses you can't quite explain, deadlines that keep slipping, and a team that's always behind.

Here are five signs your current process is costing you contracts — and what to do about each.

## 1. You Find Out About RFPs After They're Posted

If your team is discovering opportunities on SAM.gov the day they're published, you're already behind. The companies winning consistently built their pipeline before the solicitation dropped.

The best BD teams track pre-solicitation notices, Sources Sought, and RFIs months in advance. By the time the RFP is live, they've already shaped requirements, built agency relationships, and started drafting key sections.

**Fix it:** Set up automated alerts for pre-solicitation activity in your target agencies, not just final RFPs.

## 2. Your Compliance Matrix Is Built Manually

If someone on your team is reading a 300-page solicitation line by line and building a spreadsheet from scratch, you're spending 20–40 hours on work that should take 20 minutes.

A compliance matrix is not a differentiator. It's table stakes. The time your team spends building one manually is time not spent writing the technical approach that actually wins the award.

**Fix it:** Automate compliance matrix generation. Every requirement should be captured and cross-referenced before a human writer touches it.

## 3. You're Rewriting the Same Content Every Bid

Look at your last five proposals. How much of the company overview, past performance section, and management approach is identical? If your team is rewriting from scratch each time, you don't have a content problem — you have a library problem.

High-performing proposal teams maintain a content library of pre-approved, winning language organized by section, topic, and NAICS code. New proposals pull from that library and customize for the specific opportunity.

**Fix it:** After every proposal, archive winning sections. Build a searchable content library. Stop starting from zero.

## 4. Your Win Rate Isn't Tracked

If you don't know your win rate by agency, contract type, or dollar value, you can't improve it.

Most teams track wins and losses anecdotally. The data exists — FPDS, USASpending, your own CRM — but nobody's aggregating it into an actionable picture.

**Fix it:** At minimum, track: opportunities pursued, proposals submitted, awards won, and estimated contract value. Review monthly.

## 5. Your Team Is Always in Crisis Mode

If every submission feels like a sprint to the finish, the problem isn't the deadline — it's the process upstream. Proposal teams that are always in crisis mode have an intake and scheduling problem, not a writing problem.

Rushed proposals have thinner technical approaches, weaker past performance narratives, and more compliance errors. Evaluators can tell.

**Fix it:** Build backward from the deadline. If the RFP closes in 30 days, your outline should be done in 3, your first draft in 15, your review in 22, and your final in 28. Anything outside that rhythm is a warning sign.

All five of these signs share the same root cause: a process built on manual effort and institutional memory rather than systems and data. The teams winning the most contracts aren't working harder — they're working inside a better machine.

## What Is a Bid/No-Bid Decision?

A bid/no-bid decision is a structured go/no-go evaluation you run before committing resources to an RFP response. The goal is simple: determine whether the opportunity is worth pursuing *before* you spend a dollar on it.

Most companies skip this or do it informally. A BD lead glances at the requirements, checks the budget, and gives a gut-call. That's not a framework — that's guessing.

## The 5-Question Bid/No-Bid Framework

Run every opportunity through these five questions. Score each 1–3. Any total under 10 is a no-bid.

**1. Do we meet the stated requirements?**
Can you check every mandatory requirement in the RFP? Not close — every single one. If there are gaps, score this a 1.

**2. Do we have an incumbent disadvantage?**
Is there a company already doing this work? If yes, do you have a clear differentiator that beats their past performance advantage? Score 1 if you're bidding blind against an incumbent.

**3. Is the budget realistic for our cost structure?**
Government contracts often have ceiling prices buried in the solicitation or visible in prior awards. If the budget doesn't work at your margin, it's a 1.

**4. Do we have relevant past performance?**
Government evaluators score past performance heavily. Do you have 2–3 examples of directly comparable work? If not, score 2 at best.

**5. Is there time to write a competitive response?**
A rushed proposal is a losing proposal. Do you have enough runway to be thorough? If you're looking at a 10-day turnaround with no existing content, score this a 1.

**Scoring:**
- 13–15: Strong bid — commit resources
- 10–12: Conditional bid — proceed with a plan to address weaknesses
- Under 10: No-bid — document why and move on

## Why Most Teams Get This Wrong

The pressure to bid comes from the top. Revenue targets, pipeline pressure, and FOMO push teams to pursue opportunities they shouldn't. The framework only works if leadership respects the no-bid decision.

Build a simple rule: every no-bid decision gets documented with a one-line reason. This creates a record that helps you learn over time which signals predict wins — and which predict losses.
